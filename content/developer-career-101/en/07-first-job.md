# Developer Career 101 (7/10): Settling into the First Job

The first developer job often creates pressure to prove yourself immediately. Most teams are not looking for instant mastery in the first ninety days. They are looking for how fast you learn the context, how clearly you ask for help, and whether you can turn that help into small reliable contributions.

This is the 7th post in the Developer Career 101 series.


![developer career 101 chapter 7 flow overview](https://yeongseon-books.github.io/book-public-assets/assets/developer-career-101/07/07-01-concept-at-a-glance.en.png)

> Your first job is about proving you can complete assigned work well and learning how your team works, not proving you are the smartest person in the room.

## Questions to Keep in Mind

- What should a new developer focus on during the first ninety days?
- Why do questions, notes, and feedback loops matter more than pretending to know everything?
- How can your first PR and your 1:1s build trust quickly?

## What You Will Learn

- *Onboarding* strategy
- The *question* technique
- Small *contributions*
- The *feedback loop*
- Building *relationships*

## Why It Matters

First impressions frame the collaboration dynamic for roughly the next eighteen months. How you learn and communicate early becomes the floor for growth speed and trust.

> The first ninety days are less about proving capability and more about demonstrating how you learn and how you collaborate.

## The Big Picture

Good onboarding is not a solo endurance test. Learning first, asking questions, shipping small contributions, and correcting course through feedback—this loop is what lets you find your footing quickly.

## Key Terms

- **onboarding**: Adaptation period for the organization and codebase.
- **buddy**: Peer mentor who helps day-to-day.
- **1:1**: One-on-one meeting.
- **shadow**: Observe alongside a colleague.
- **psychological safety**: Sense of safety to speak up without penalty.

## Before/After

**Before**: "I study by myself only."

**After**: "I batch questions weekly and ship a PR."

## Hands-on: A Ninety-Day Plan

### Step 1 — Day 30: Learn

```text
- explore the codebase
- shadow an oncall
- write a glossary
```

The first thirty days are about building broad context, not rushing to deliver. Scanning the codebase structure, observing on-call or deployment flows, and writing a team glossary raises the quality of your questions.

### Step 2 — Day 60: Curate Questions

```markdown
## question notebook
- why is X faster than Y?
- what is Z's retention policy?
```

Questions scatter easily. Collecting them in a notebook reduces duplicates and lets you ask shorter, sharper questions in 1:1s and buddy meetings.

### Step 3 — Day 90: First PR

```bash
git checkout -b first-fix
# small, scoped change
```

The smaller the first PR, the better. Starting with a scoped bug fix or documentation improvement means faster reviews and quicker trust accumulation.

### Step 4 — Use 1:1s

```text
weekly with manager, biweekly with mentor
```

A 1:1 is not a status report—it is a feedback session. Instead of letting worries pile up, bring them structured from your question notebook.

### Step 5 — Retro

```markdown
- went well: code reading
- gap: question frequency
- next: one question per day
```

An onboarding retro surfaces what went well and what fell short quickly. Small course corrections early produce outsized differences in perceived growth speed.

## Ninety-Day Operating Plan

| Window | Priority | Useful artifact |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Days 0–30 | Build context and language | Team glossary, system notes |
| Days 31–60 | Improve question quality | Question notebook, 1:1 agenda |
| Days 61–90 | Ship small and learn fast | First PR, retro notes |
| Entire period | Surface confusion early | Check-in rhythm and help requests |

## 1:1 Meeting Structure

At your first job, 1:1s are not status reports—they are direction-correction devices. Especially during the first ninety days, they are the primary channel for raising question quality and learning speed, so having a structure helps.

| Segment | Time | Core question | Preparation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Check-in | 5 min | What blocked me most this week? | Question notebook |
| Progress share | 10 min | What risks exist in current work? | Task board |
| Feedback request | 10 min | What should I fix in my communication/priorities? | PR examples |
| Next action | 5 min | What is my one action for next week? | Action item |

Effective 1:1 participants typically organize problems in the order "current state → why blocked → what I tried." Following this order lets managers or mentors give actionable feedback immediately.

## Performance Review Self-Assessment Examples

A self-assessment is not a document bragging about your accomplishments. It is a document aligning evidence to team expectations.

| Category | Weak version | Strong version |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Execution | "I developed several features." | "Released 3 features within the quarter and reduced incident recurrence by 20 %." |
| Collaboration | "I did reviews diligently." | "Introduced a review template that shortened PR round-trip time by an average of 1.4 days." |
| Learning | "I studied new technologies." | "Wrote on-call retros twice a month and updated 6 team runbooks." |

## Trust-Building Behavior Principles

- Share what you do not know early rather than hiding it until late.
- Before asking for help, summarize what you have already tried.
- Keep small promises to build an impression of being a "predictable colleague."
- When you receive feedback, connect it to a visible action next week.

Early trust comes from consistent fundamentals more than big achievements.

## 12-Week Execution Timeline

What accelerates growth at a first job is not "working hard" but "predictable execution." The timeline below is a basic frame for a junior engineer operating through the first twelve weeks.

| Period | Goal | Core actions | Deliverable |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Weeks 1–2 | Understand the system | Write codebase map, automate local setup | 1 architecture memo |
| Weeks 3–4 | Start small contributions | Doc fixes + 2 bug fixes | 3+ PRs |
| Weeks 5–8 | Feature-level ownership | Develop a small feature, deploy end-to-end | Feature retro doc |
| Weeks 9–12 | Secure team trust | Participate in incident response, complete 1:1 actions | 1 improvement proposal |

The key is not "big achievements" but "repetition of small commitments." For example, if you increase review feedback turnaround speed during weeks 3–4, the team trusts your collaboration stability before your technical ability. First-job adaptation is a process of building a trust circuit, not demonstrating technical perfection.

## 1:1 Meeting Template (Manager/Mentor)

For the first six months, fixing a biweekly 1:1 cadence works well. The template below is for a 25-minute session.

| Segment | Question | Evidence to prepare |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Performance check | What did I contribute to the team in the last two weeks? | PR links, deployment records |
| Bottleneck share | What area am I repeatedly stuck on? | Error logs, code locations |
| Relationship check | Were there any collaboration misunderstandings? | Communication notes |
| Next action | What is the one risk I will eliminate in the next two weeks? | 3-line execution plan |

After the meeting, register action items in your calendar immediately. Leaving them as "let's do it next time" means they never happen. At a first job, 1:1s are not performance reviews—they are operational devices that reduce the cost of trial and error.

## Performance Review Criteria Table (Junior Level)

Performance reviews look at reproducibility more than the size of deliverables. The table below is useful for self-checks before a half-year review.

| Category | Expected standard | Self-check question |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Execution | 80 %+ on-time completion rate for committed work | Can I explain the root cause of repeated delays? |
| Code quality | Decreasing trend in review revision counts | Are the same review comments recurring? |
| Operational sense | Records of incident/issue response participation | Can I narrow down causes using logs and metrics? |
| Collaboration | Document-based communication | Did I leave decision rationale in writing? |

Writing self-assessments based on this table lets you show "what changed" rather than "I worked hard."

## Onboarding Checklist: Routines for Earning Team Trust

First-job onboarding is less about knowledge acquisition and more about matching the team's alignment cadence. Checking this list weekly accelerates adaptation.

- Did I fill in gaps in the dev environment setup docs?
- Did I incorporate review feedback within the same week?
- Did I document unfamiliar terms in the team wiki?
- Did I run the pre-deploy checklist myself?

Repeating these four regardless of technical proficiency quickly earns the trust of being "a comfortable colleague to work with."

## Communication Template: Status Sharing

At a first job, missing status updates creates more distrust than technical mistakes. Sharing daily status in this format raises collaboration stability.

- Completed today: one line on what was finished
- Current risk: one line on what is blocked and why
- Request: one line on who you need help from and what kind
- Next plan: one line on the next task to finish today

Short as it looks, this routine lets the team predict progress and lets reviewers step in at the right time.

## Criteria for Picking Early Wins

In the first weeks, rather than picking high-difficulty tasks, solve the repetitive problems the team faces frequently. For example, deploy check misses, excessive test run times, or documentation drift are all clearly scoped and produce immediately visible improvement. Completing two or three such tasks proves not just technical skill but execution reliability.

## First Six Months Risk Management Table

| Risk | Early signal | Response action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Priority confusion | Same task repeatedly postponed | Weekly priority re-alignment with manager |
| Quality degradation | Review revisions keep repeating | Self-review with checklist before submission |
| Relationship disconnect | Help requests come late | Restore daily status sharing routine |

The later you discover a risk, the higher the adaptation cost. Managing early signals in a table accelerates response.

## Weekly Retro Sentence Template

Keep retros to three core sentences rather than writing long-form. Recording "value I created this week," "repeated mistake," and "risk I will eliminate next week" every week naturally accumulates growth evidence.

## What to Notice in This Code

- Questions accelerate learning.

## Five Common Mistakes

1. **Hiding what you do not know.**
2. **Questions too large.**
3. **No PRs.**
4. **Skipping 1:1s.**
5. **No notes.**

## How This Shows Up in Production

Many companies use ninety-day goals and a first PR as official onboarding metrics. Learning speed and collaboration attitude are clearly observed from the very start.

## How a Senior Engineer Thinks

- Asking is courtesy, not imposing.
- Small PRs are progress.
- 1:1s are trust-building time.
- Notes are career assets.
- Observation is learning.

## Wrap-up and Next Steps

A good start at your first job does not come from enduring alone. Building a loop of learning, asking, contributing small things, and adjusting through feedback is the core. The next post covers principles for running side projects that do not collapse alongside your main job.

## Answering the Opening Questions

- **What should you focus on during the first 90 days at your first developer job?**
  - The most important thing at your first job is completing assigned work accurately and quickly learning the team's way of working.
- **Why is asking questions and documenting more important than pretending to know a lot?**
  - Code review culture, testing practices, deployment procedures, and tech stack decisions differ at every company — you need an attitude of respecting and learning these.
- **How do first PRs and 1:1s become trust-building mechanisms?**
  - If you can concretely express your growth and difficulties in a 1:1 with a senior after 3 months, your next steps become clear.
