# Developer Career 101 (1/10): What Is a Developer Career

People often start by mapping a developer career to company names, years of experience, or the next title. In practice, two engineers with the same title can be operating at very different levels because the real difference shows up in problem scope, judgment, and how much leverage they create for other people.

This is the first post in the Developer Career 101 series.


![developer career 101 chapter 1 flow overview](https://yeongseon-books.github.io/book-public-assets/assets/developer-career-101/01/01-01-concept-at-a-glance.en.png)

> Career growth has three axes: role (what level of work you own), skill (which of technical, collaborative, or domain understanding is weakest), and impact (who benefits from your work).

## Questions to Keep in Mind

- Why is a developer career easier to manage when you treat it as a growth structure rather than a title ladder?
- What actually changes between junior, mid-level, senior, and staff-like expectations?
- Why do skill, influence, and written evidence matter together instead of separately?

## Why It Matters

Without direction, five years later you are still at square one. When you look at career through the lens of role, skill, and impact, learning plans, project selection, performance reviews, and job-move decisions all connect into a single flow.

> A developer career is not a list of titles—it is a curve where role, skill, and impact grow together.

## The Big Picture

Career stages are continuous—junior to mid to senior represent growing scope of responsibility, not separate boxes. Understanding a career as a structure where multiple axes accumulate together is more accurate than seeing it as a staircase of titles.

## Key Terms

- **junior**: Works from instructions and context provided by others.
- **mid**: Structures work independently and drives it forward.
- **senior**: Defines problems and explains tradeoffs of different solutions.
- **lead**: Owns team-level outcomes.
- **principal**: Shapes organization-level direction.

## Before/After

**Before**: "I work only for the next promotion."

**After**: "I sketch a skill curve and grow my impact radius."

## Hands-on: Plot Your Career Coordinate

### Step 1 — Current Stage

```text
one of: junior / mid / senior
```

Write based on how you work, not your official title. What matters is whether you execute instructions, set your own priorities, or define problems.

### Step 2 — Core Skills

```text
- technical
- collaborative
- domain
```

Splitting into technical depth, collaboration, and domain understanding reveals whether growth is lopsided. You may be strong technically but weak at collaboration, or great at explaining things but shallow in depth.

### Step 3 — Impact Radius

```text
- self
- team
- org
- industry
```

Mark how far your work currently reaches. Whether you solve things alone, boost team productivity, or shift organizational standards determines your next goal.

### Step 4 — Six-Month Goals

```markdown
- one technical depth
- one new domain
- one talk delivered
```

Mix goals across different types. Combining one depth target, one new domain, and one externally visible deliverable keeps your career moving in a balanced way.

### Step 5 — Quarterly Retro

```markdown
## Q2 retro
- went well
- gaps
- one thing for next quarter
```

A retro is not a diary entry—it is a document that decides your next action. Writing down what went well, where the gaps are, and one thing to change next quarter sharpens your sense of direction.

## Decision Frame for Reading Your Own Career

| Axis | Question to ask now | Useful next move |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Role | Are you mostly executing assigned work, or are you defining the work too? | Review the last four weeks and count how many tasks included framing, not just implementation. |
| Skill | Which of technical depth, collaboration, or domain understanding is weakest? | Pick the weakest axis and connect one concrete deliverable to it this quarter. |
| Impact | Who gets faster or safer because of your work besides you? | Leave one reusable artifact behind: a doc, review pattern, template, or automation. |
| Record | Could you prove your growth in the next review loop? | Start a weekly work log and a monthly retro. |

## Career Track Comparison

A developer career splits into several tracks: IC (Individual Contributor), Manager, Architect, and Startup. Each track demands different skills and offers different reward structures.

| Track | Core competency | Reward structure | Growth path |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| IC (Individual Contributor) | Technical depth, code quality, problem-solving speed | Salary + stock/bonus tied to technical level | Junior → Mid → Senior → Staff → Principal |
| Manager | Team performance, people management, org communication | Salary + bonus tied to team size and results | Tech Lead → Engineering Manager → Director → VP |
| Architect | System design, technical strategy, standards | Salary + project-based, decision-making authority | Senior → Architect → Principal Architect → CTO |
| Startup | Business sense, product intuition, fundraising | Equity + market value, high risk and high reward | Solo Founder → Co-Founder → CEO + CTO combined |

The IC track grows through code and technical depth. Managers are evaluated on team performance and communication. Architects are responsible for system-wide consistency and technical strategy. Startups trade responsibility for the entire product and business in exchange for outsized risk and reward.

## Expected Competencies by Stage

From junior to staff, what companies expect expands progressively.

### Junior (0–2 years)

Works from instructions and context. Execution stability matters more than breadth.

- Ability to turn assigned tickets into working code
- Understanding and incorporating code review feedback
- Asking questions at the right time when blocked

### Mid (3–5 years)

Structures work independently. Owns features end-to-end, beyond individual tickets.

- Decomposing requirements into an implementation plan
- Completing testing and deployment independently
- Collaborating with teammates and providing code reviews

### Senior (6–10 years)

Defines problems and explains tradeoffs. Looks beyond a single feature to the system as a whole.

- Turning ambiguous requirements into clear problem definitions
- Explaining tradeoffs and making technical decisions
- Mentoring juniors and mids

### Staff (10+ years)

Shapes organization-level direction. Defines technical strategy and culture across multiple teams.

- Drawing the technology roadmap for an entire organization
- Mediating decision conflicts across multiple teams
- Documenting and propagating technical standards

## Salary Negotiation Reference Points

Salary negotiation is a data-driven decision, not an emotional one. Prepare these three things before sitting at the table.

### 1. Market Data

Collect market salary data for your role, experience, and location.

```python
# Example: checking market salary range
position = "Senior Backend Engineer"
years = 7
location = "Seoul"

# Data collected from public compensation platforms and job sites
market_range = {
    "p25": 80_000_000,  # bottom 25%
    "p50": 100_000_000, # median
    "p75": 120_000_000  # top 25%
}

# Setting your target range
target_min = market_range["p50"]
target_max = market_range["p75"]
print(f"Target range: {target_min:,} ~ {target_max:,} KRW")
```

### 2. Performance Evidence

At the negotiation table, concrete numbers beat abstract claims.

- API response time improved from 200 ms → 80 ms
- Deployment cycle shortened from two weeks → three days
- Team code coverage raised from 40 % → 75 %

### 3. Alternative Offers

Having another offer dramatically increases leverage. Even ongoing interviews give you something to mention.

```text
"I am also in the final stages with Company A and Company B.
Your company is my top priority, but I need to consider all options."
```

Negotiation opportunities are often one-shot. Instead of throwing out a number unprepared, arrive at the table armed with market data, performance evidence, and alternative offers.

## Job Type Comparison for Choosing a Starting Point

The most common mistake when starting a career is assuming "all developer jobs are basically the same." In reality, even at the same experience level, problem types, collaboration structures, and success metrics differ significantly. Understanding the texture of each role at the starting line keeps your learning plan and project choices from drifting. The table below compares role types that beginners often deliberate between.

| Job type | Typical problems | Early core skills | Early deliverables | Mid-term expansion |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Frontend | UI flows, rendering performance, accessibility | HTML/CSS/JS basics, state management intuition | Feature PRs, performance improvement logs | Design systems, frontend architecture |
| Backend | API design, data consistency, incident response | HTTP/DB/transaction understanding | API implementations, monitoring metrics | Domain modeling, distributed systems sense |
| Data | ETL pipelines, analysis accuracy, metric reliability | SQL, data modeling, validation habits | Report automation, pipeline documentation | Data platforms, governance |
| SRE/Platform | Availability, deployment stability, operational automation | Linux, observability, incident response | Runbooks, alert improvements, ops scripts | SLO design, operational culture |

The table reveals that "what type of failure you reduce" distinguishes roles more clearly than tech stacks do. When choosing your fit, look at your reaction to failure types rather than language preferences. Someone who enjoys spending hours polishing UI quality has high frontend affinity; someone who relentlessly traces data consistency bugs tends to grow quickly in backend or data roles.

## How to Read a Career Path Diagram

Career path diagrams usually appear as a straight line—"Junior → Mid → Senior"—but in practice three axes move simultaneously.

1. Role axis: shifts from execution-centric to problem-definition-centric.
2. Skill axis: balances technical depth, collaboration, and domain understanding.
3. Impact axis: expands from personal productivity to team productivity to organizational standards.

Many assume influence grows automatically with tenure. In reality, "reusable traces" like documentation, reviews, and automation are required for the impact axis to climb. A career path diagram should be read not as a tenure graph but as a roadmap for increasing repeatable contribution patterns.

## 12-Month Growth Plan Example

Below is a realistic growth plan for the first year.

| Period | Goal | Execution criteria | Verification |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Months 1–3 | Adapt to the codebase | 5 code-reading notes per week | 2 mentor reviews |
| Months 4–6 | Feature-level ownership | Lead 1 feature per month | PR quality / review turnaround |
| Months 7–9 | Build operational sense | Participate in 2 incident retros | Retro document contributions |
| Months 10–12 | Start expanding influence | 1 tech note or internal share | Team feedback record |

The key is converting every goal into a visible artifact. "Study hard" cannot be verified, but "one monthly tech note" can. A career is ultimately an accumulation of repeatedly verifiable actions.

## Job Posting Interpretation Template

When reading a job posting, do not check off the technology list literally. Decompose the business context first. The same "Python" requirement means very different things depending on whether the team operates data pipelines or builds API products. The template below is a format you can paste and use when analyzing a posting.

| Item | Question | Example notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Product stage | Is this team at 0→1 or 1→10? | Established B2B SaaS with many customers, stabilization phase |
| Key metric | What is the team trying to move? | Payment conversion rate, batch success rate |
| Technical difficulty | Is scalability, performance, or data accuracy the core challenge? | Traffic variability handling, p95 target 350 ms |
| Collaboration structure | What is the interface with PM/Design/Data? | Weekly sprint planning, daily standup |
| Growth opportunity | What problems will you be solving in one year? | From service ops to architecture improvements |

After filling this table, compute a fit score. Rate work interest, current skill match, growth potential, and compensation fit on a 5-point scale each; apply only to postings averaging 3.8 or above. This threshold reduces the anxiety-driven pattern of mass applications.

## How to Read Salary Survey Tables

Salary negotiation is data-driven decision-making. Reading ranges matters more than absolute numbers. Below is an example of range data collected from public channels for a junior backend role.

| Data source | Bottom 25 % | Median | Top 25 % | Notes |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Domestic job platform A | 42 M KRW | 49 M KRW | 58 M KRW | High startup share |
| Domestic job platform B | 45 M KRW | 52 M KRW | 61 M KRW | Series B+ companies dominant |
| Global compensation platform | 50 M KRW | 60 M KRW | 74 M KRW | Includes foreign/remote |

Do not jump straight to quoting a desired salary. First, categorize your situation into three bands:
- Base band: high risk, e.g., role switch or new grad
- Standard band: meet 70 %+ of required skills
- Stretch band: can demonstrate immediate combat-readiness in a specific skill

Then prepare a negotiation sentence. Saying "Given my experience and the current market range, the 52–58 M KRW band feels reasonable—though I am open to adjusting based on responsibility scope and on-call load" frames the conversation around a range and puts you in the driver's seat.

## 12-Month Career Roadmap Timeline

Career design needs quarterly execution plans more than a single long-term goal. The timeline below is a good reference for a junior developer operating through the first year.

| Period | Core goal | Deliverable | Verification criteria |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Months 1–3 | Lock in execution routines | Weekly learning log, 1 mini-project | Maintain 6+ hours of study per week |
| Months 4–6 | Strengthen portfolio credibility | 1 deployed project, 2 tech posts | Code review feedback applied |
| Months 7–9 | Build interview readiness | 60 algorithm problems, 4 mock interviews | 60 % solve rate within 45 min |
| Months 10–12 | Prepare for improvement / job move | Analysis table of 15 target companies, application docs | 20 %+ resume pass rate |

The key in timeline operation is not reducing tasks but clarifying priority. For example, during months 4–6, instead of adding new technologies, extending an existing project with tests, documentation, and monitoring raises completion quality—which pays off more in the long run. Hiring managers value the connection between problem definition, implementation, and operation more than the count of technology names.

## Understanding the Promotion Criteria Matrix First

When designing a career, focusing only on job-hopping and salary can cause you to miss growth opportunities at your current company. Promotion criteria are typically defined not by technical ability alone but by problem-solving scope and influence.

| Level | Expected role | Evidence examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Junior | Solve assigned problems accurately | Review turnaround speed, defect reduction |
| Mid | Decompose ambiguous requirements and solve them | Design memos, feature lead experience |
| Senior | Improve team execution through structural changes | Operational metric improvements, mentoring outcomes |

Knowing these criteria in advance changes your daily learning choices. Rather than learning yet another framework, a single automation that removes your team's bottleneck may be a stronger growth signal.

Keeping a monthly career decision log gradually improves your judgment quality.

| Question | This month's answer | Next month's check item |
| --- | --- | --- |
| What is my biggest growth bottleneck right now? | Lack of operational experience | Request 1 on-call shadow session |

## Monthly Check Questions

At the end of every month, run through the same set of questions to check your career direction. Confirming whether your current work will still be an asset one year from now—and whether you can clearly articulate the achievement you will prove next quarter—reduces directional drift.

## Answering the Opening Questions

- **Why should you view a developer career as a growth structure rather than a sequence of job titles?**
  - You need to look at three axes: role (what level of work you handle), competency (which of technical/collaboration/domain is weakest), and impact (who benefits from your work).
- **What actually changes when stages like junior, mid, and senior are divided?**
  - Execution following directions (junior), self-directed prioritization (mid), and judgment in defining problems and explaining tradeoffs (senior).
- **Why are impact and documentation important alongside technical capability?**
  - Technical skill alone limits what you can do solo, but documentation and automation extend your impact, and records serve evaluations and job transitions.
